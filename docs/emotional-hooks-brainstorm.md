# Emotional Hooks

What makes Cull feel like something people *want* to use, not just another utility.

---

### 1. "Nothing leaves your phone"

Zero networking code. No analytics. No cloud. Photos are referenced by pointer, never copied. This isn't a privacy *policy* — it's a privacy *architecture*. In a world where every photo app feels like it's watching you, Cull simply can't. The hook: **trust through absence**.

---

### 2. "Your camera roll, without the chaos"

The swipe-to-organize loop turns a dreaded chore into something almost meditative. The key detail: there are no "wrong" answers. Dismiss, sort, skip, favorite — every action is undoable, and dismissed photos aren't even deleted until you explicitly say so. The app is patient with indecision.

---

### 3. "Swiping as muscle memory"

Left, right, up, down — each direction has a meaning, confirmed by a subtle haptic pulse and a colored flash. No buttons to hunt for, no menus to open. After 30 seconds, your hands know the system. This is the Tinder insight: **the gesture disappears, and you're just making decisions**.

---

### 4. "Going back in time"

The date picker isn't just a filter — it's a time machine. "From the very first photo." "On This Day." You're not opening a tool, you're choosing which chapter of your life to revisit. The photo's date is displayed at the bottom of each card — a quiet anchor to *when that moment happened*.

---

### 5. "No guilt, no pressure"

- Full undo stack (not just last action — the whole session)
- "Dismissed" not "Deleted" — language that implies second chances
- Focus timer says "Keep Going" or "Done" — never "You didn't finish"
- Session summary shows what you *accomplished*, not what's left
- Double-tap to skip means "not now" is always an option

The emotional message: **you can't mess this up**.

---

### 6. "Your galleries are yours"

Custom names, custom colors, custom icons, custom order. When you swipe right and a photo lands in a glowing neon panel labeled with *your* word — "Family," "Sunsets," "Chaos" — that's not file management. That's **curating your own life**.

---

### 7. "A fresh start every time"

The random session accent color is a small detail with outsized emotional weight. Each launch feels slightly different. It says: **this isn't a grind with a progress bar. It's a new session, a new color, a new mood.**

---

## App Personality

**In one sentence:** Cull is an intimate, unhurried space where you reconnect with your memories one photo at a time.

It's not playful (no gamification, no streaks), not serious (no productivity metrics), and not minimalist just for aesthetics — it's **quiet**. The minimalism serves a purpose: getting out of the way so the user can feel something when a forgotten photo surfaces. The swipe gesture is almost meditative — one memory, one decision, no rush.

If the competitors feel like "cleaning out your closet," Cull should feel like "flipping through an old photo album on a Sunday afternoon."

---

## Color Palette Philosophy

Two layers:

**The canvas is quiet.** System backgrounds, semantic text colors, glass effects. Intentionally neutral — it disappears so the photo stays the hero.

**The accents are electric.** A 10-color neon palette — hot pink, bright blue, neon green, electric purple, vivid yellow, orange, cyan, deep red, lime, magenta — each with hand-tuned light and dark mode variants. Every session randomly picks one as the app-wide tint, and each gallery gets its own.

**Neutral until it matters, then unmistakably vivid.**

---

## Lead Marketing Line

> **"Your memories deserve better than scrolling past them."**

Everyone has thousands of photos they never look at. Cull doesn't guilt you about it — it gives you a calm, private, five-minute ritual to actually *see* them again, one at a time, and decide what matters.

---

## Pricing

**$2.99 USD, one-time purchase. No in-app purchases, no subscriptions, no tip jars.**

- Competitors charge $3.99/month (Slidebox, Swipewipe). A one-time $2.99 is a statement: "Pay once, own everything, forever." Consistent with "no upgrade to unlock."
- Free signals "hobby project" or "you're the product." A small price signals someone cared. It builds the same trust that the no-analytics architecture does.
- $2.99 is impulse-buy territory — below the psychological $5 threshold.
- The receipt is the last time the app asks anything of you. That's consistent with "complete from zero moment" — the zero moment shifts to after purchase instead of after download.
- Still open source. Code on GitHub, paid on the App Store. People pay for the convenience and the polish, not the source code.
